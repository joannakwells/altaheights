Manifesto / Alta Heights

Alta Heights is a small on purpose, fiercely independent operation. In response to growing distributor consolidation, corporate acquisitions, incentivized reviews and paid influencers, we decided to pick our fighter – the independent voices that keep wine exciting. This page is our argument for why that matters — and why, once you go up the mountain, it's very hard to come back down.

The Independent Standard

  • Know where it came from.

    Not just the region — the vineyard, the elevation, the farmer. Real independent wine has a traceable origin that someone is proud of.

  • Taste the place.

    If it could have come from anywhere, it probably did. Mountain wine tastes like the mountain: mineral, brisk, specific. You should be able to close your eyes and see the terrain.

  • Reject the smooth.

    Corporate wine is engineered to eliminate edges. Independent wine keeps them. An interesting glass of wine has something to say — texture, tension, a little resistance.

  • Support the farmer, not the brand.

    Most famous wine labels are owned by conglomerates. When you buy independent, the money goes to the people who grew it — and they use it to keep growing it the way they believe in.

  • Drink it outside.

    Not a requirement. Just a strong suggestion. Mountain wine was designed — spiritually, at least — for open air, good company, and somewhere with a view.

The Valley Floor Problem

Ninety percent of California wine comes from flat land optimized for tonnage.

Valley floor vineyards are efficient. They're irrigated, mechanically harvested, and yield-optimized. The grapes ripen fast and easy. The resulting wine is technically correct and commercially comfortable — and it floods every retail shelf, restaurant list, and grocery aisle in the country.

The mountain vineyard operates on different rules entirely. Steep, rocky, often organic by necessity. Hand-farmed. Lower yields. Longer hang time. The vine is stressed; the grape is concentrated. You taste the work.

"To grow grapes and produce wines in the mountains, you have to be a lot more dedicated. Most of us do it because we love it."

— Ken Burnap, Santa Cruz Mountain Vineyard, 1979

What Mountain Wine Actually Is

Alive, specific, and slightly inconvenient to make.

Mountain wine isn't a style — it's a condition. It's what happens when a small team of people decides to grow grapes in a difficult place for reasons that have nothing to do with maximizing ROI.

The wines are tighter in youth, more aromatic, more food-friendly. The tannins have texture rather than grip. The acidity is real, not added. The color comes from the fruit, not from concentration. These aren't things you can engineer in a lab or replicate at scale. They emerge from place, season, and restraint.
